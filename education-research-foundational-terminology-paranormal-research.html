Basic Terminology and Foundational Language in Paranormal Research

A terminology paper for careful communication, evidence review, and public reporting.

Education and Community Development Reporting and Documentation

Abstract

Language shapes investigation. The words investigators use influence how claims are understood, how evidence is reviewed, and how findings are communicated to witnesses and the public.

This paper establishes the importance of foundational terminology and careful language in paranormal research.

Why Language Matters

Words such as "reported," "observed," "possible," "plausible," "unresolved," and "inconclusive" preserve accuracy. Words such as "confirmed," "definitely haunted," "demonic," or "spirit attachment" can create harm when used without sufficient support.

Careful language does not weaken an investigation. It strengthens it by matching the conclusion to the evidence.

Observation, Claim, and Conclusion

An observation is what was directly noticed or recorded. A claim is what someone says the observation means. A conclusion is the investigator's carefully supported interpretation after review.

Confusing these categories is one of the most common causes of poor reporting. "A door opened" is not the same as "a spirit opened the door." The first can be documented. The second requires a much higher evidentiary standard.

Possible and Plausible

Possible means an explanation cannot be ruled out. Plausible means the explanation fits the available evidence better than competing explanations after reasonable review. Neither term should be used casually.

These terms allow investigators to remain open to anomalous possibilities without overstating what has been demonstrated.

Conclusion

Terminology is part of methodology. When investigators use careful language, they protect witnesses, preserve evidence, and strengthen the credibility of the field.
