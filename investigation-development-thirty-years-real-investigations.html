Thirty Years In The Field

Thirty years in this field has taught me many things.

One of the biggest lessons has very little to do with ghosts. It has to do with people.

I have spent more than three decades investigating homes, businesses, historical locations, and listening to people who were simply looking for answers.

Most of that work happened quietly. There were no television cameras. No large audiences. No ticket sales. No viral videos.

Just real people. Real investigations. Real questions.

Visibility Is Not The Same As Experience

Over the years I reached out to magazines, symposiums, organizations, and others within the paranormal community hoping to contribute what I had learned from hundreds of investigations.

More often than not, I never heard anything back.

For a long time I wondered why. Today, I think I understand.

The paranormal field often rewards visibility more than experience. Followers more than field work. Entertainment more than investigation.

That is not true of everyone, but it has become increasingly common.

The Quiet Work Matters

Some of the finest investigators I have ever met are not on television.

They do not have hundreds of thousands of followers. They are not chasing the newest haunted attraction every weekend.

They are quietly helping families. They are documenting cases. They are researching. They are asking difficult questions. They are admitting when they do not know the answer.

Unfortunately, many of those voices are rarely heard.

Experience does not always trend. Integrity does not always go viral. Careful documentation does not usually generate millions of views.

But those things matter.

There Is Room For Everyone

There is absolutely nothing wrong with visiting haunted locations, exploring local legends, or enjoying the entertainment side of the paranormal.

Many of us do exactly that, and those experiences are part of what brings people into this field.

The problem begins when popularity becomes a substitute for experience. When followers become more important than field work. When confidence replaces curiosity.

The paranormal community has room for everyone: researchers, investigators, explorers, historians, storytellers, content creators, and people who simply enjoy the mystery.

Every one of those perspectives has value.

What We Should Value

If we are serious about moving this field forward, perhaps it is time we place a little more value on knowledge, documentation, honesty, and decades of real-world experience, not just algorithms.

I have never wanted fame. I never needed television.

What I have always wanted is to share what thirty years has taught me with anyone willing to learn.

Why The Paranormal Initiative Exists

That is why The Paranormal Initiative exists.

Not to tell people what to believe, but to share experience, encourage exploration, ask better questions, and help raise the standard for everyone who loves this field.

If even one person becomes a better investigator because of something I have shared, then every one of those thirty years has been worth it.
