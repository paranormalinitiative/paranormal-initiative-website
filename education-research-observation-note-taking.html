Basic Observation and Note-Taking

A documentation paper on preserving observations before interpretation changes them.

Reporting and Documentation Investigation Science

Abstract

Observation and note-taking are foundational investigative skills. They preserve what happened before memory, excitement, fear, or group discussion changes the account.

This paper explains how basic notes protect evidence, reduce contamination, and support later review.

Observation Before Interpretation

An investigator should first record what was seen, heard, felt, measured, or captured. Interpretation comes later. "Three knocks were heard from the east wall at 9:42 PM" is stronger than "a spirit knocked."

Separating observation from interpretation allows other investigators to review the event without being forced into a conclusion.

What to Record

Useful notes include time, location, people present, equipment active, weather, room conditions, sounds, lighting, odors, movement, device readings, and immediate possible explanations.

Notes should also record what did not happen. Silence, failed attempts to recreate, lack of repeated response, and unchanged baseline conditions may all matter.

Preserving the Original Account

Witness statements should be documented as close to the original wording as possible. Investigator assumptions should not be inserted into the witness's language.

When reviewing later, clearly mark analysis separately from the original observation.

Conclusion

Good notes are not busywork. They are the memory of the investigation. Without documentation, even a compelling experience becomes difficult to evaluate responsibly.
