Introduction to Audio, Photo, and Video Review

A media-review paper on original-file preservation, contamination control, careful interpretation, and evidence integrity.

Evidence Science and Analysis Reporting and Documentation

Introduction

An investigation may generate hundreds or even thousands of media files. A single night of recording can produce hours of audio, dozens of photographs, and multiple video clips. When the team packs up and leaves the location, the evidence review has not yet ended. In many ways, it has just begun.

This is a critical distinction. The investigation itself is the data-gathering phase. The review is where that data becomes meaningful, weak, unresolved, or unusable. Media review requires patience, organization, and a willingness to be wrong. It requires listening to uneventful audio, examining ordinary photographs, and watching video footage that may show nothing unusual.

The purpose of review is not to find the most exciting interpretation. The purpose is to document what is actually present, preserve the original record, compare the media against context, and interpret only as far as the evidence allows.

Evidence Standards for Media Review

Evidence is not the same thing as an interesting file. An interesting file may contain a strange sound, an odd shadow, a moving light, a blurred shape, or a voice-like pattern. Evidence is a file that has been preserved, described, compared, contextualized, and reviewed through a documented process.

A recording that is renamed randomly, edited destructively, shared online before review, or separated from its notes loses value. A photograph without capture context loses value. A video clip without the minutes before and after the claimed event loses value. Organization and preservation are not administrative chores. They are part of evidence integrity.

Media should be classified before it is claimed. Useful categories include explained, likely explained, unresolved, unusable, and needs further review. Explained means the likely source has been identified. Likely explained means the ordinary explanation is strong but not fully confirmed. Unresolved means a cause has not been identified after reasonable review. Unusable means the file cannot support a claim because it is too contaminated, incomplete, distorted, compressed, cropped, edited, or detached from context.

Unresolved does not mean paranormal. It means the available information does not identify the cause. The cause may still be ordinary. Honest review protects that uncertainty instead of turning it into a claim.

Enhancement is not magic. Zooming cannot create detail the camera did not capture. Noise reduction can remove information and create artifacts. Audio filtering can make random sounds seem more speech-like. Frame interpolation can invent motion between frames. Stabilization can change how movement appears. Processing is a tool for inspection, not a tool for manufacturing certainty.

Reviewing Audio

Audio review is one of the most time-consuming parts of evidence analysis. A single recorder can capture hours of ambient sound, and reviewing it properly requires focused attention.

The first rule of audio review is to listen to the complete recording. Context matters. A sound that seems mysterious in isolation may have an obvious explanation when heard in sequence. A voice-like sound that appears near the same time as a door closing, clothing movement, or a team member shifting position tells a different story than the same sound appearing in a quiet, controlled section.

One pass through an audio file is rarely enough. The first pass provides an overview. A second pass allows focus on unusual sections. If a potential anomaly is identified, additional passes should examine the sound itself, background noise, timing, and relationship to other recorded events.

Headphones are essential for serious review. Closed-back headphones help isolate the recording from the review environment and reveal details that speakers may mask. Volume should remain comfortable. Listening too loudly can fatigue the ears and distort judgment.

Every observation should be recorded with a timestamp. A useful note identifies the file, location, recorder, time code, and observation, such as: "Hallway recorder, 00:14:32, three tapping sounds." Timestamps allow comparison with field notes, environmental logs, and other recordings.

The original file should never be edited. Make working copies for review, clipping, or processing. The original file is the primary evidence source. Working copies are tools for analysis.

Most recorded sounds have ordinary sources. HVAC cycling, plumbing, settling wood, footsteps, doors, wind, rain, animals, distant traffic, aircraft, outside voices, device handling, and recorder noise should all be considered before unusual interpretations are assigned.

Voice-like sounds should be treated with restraint. A responsible early label is "audio anomaly pending further review." Do not label a sound as a spirit voice, direct communication, or EVP until stronger review methods have been applied.

Basic EVP Session Procedure

An EVP session is a structured audio recording period in which investigators ask questions, allow quiet space for possible responses, and later review the recording for voice-like anomalies. The purpose is not to prove communication in the moment. The purpose is to create a clean record that can be reviewed responsibly.

Begin with permission and purpose. Do not conduct EVP work unless there is permission to investigate and permission to record. In a residence or business, make sure everyone present understands that audio is being captured. Explain the purpose plainly: the team is recording the environment, asking structured questions, and reviewing the file later for unusual audio.

Choose the recording area carefully. Avoid rooms with loud appliances, running water, active televisions, nearby conversations, open windows, heavy traffic noise, or strong HVAC airflow when a quieter option is available. If a noisy area must be used because it relates to the claim, document the noise sources before the session begins.

Establish an audio baseline. Before asking questions, record one to two minutes of normal room tone. No one should whisper or ask questions during this baseline. Let the recorder capture HVAC hum, distant traffic, floor creaks, appliance cycles, outdoor sounds, and the natural noise floor of the device.

Identify everyone present. At the beginning of the recording, state the date, time, location, room, recorder position, and names of everyone present. If multiple recorders are used, identify each one by placement.

Control team noise. Whispering should not be allowed. If someone needs to speak, they should use a normal voice and identify themselves. If someone coughs, shifts in a chair, touches the recorder, bumps a table, steps on a squeaky board, or hears an outside sound, they should call it out clearly.

Use short, respectful questions. Ask one question at a time and leave ten to twenty seconds of silence before asking another. Avoid rapid-fire questioning, insults, taunts, threats, fear-based language, and religious or demonic provocation. Provocation is not investigation.

Keep sessions short and well documented. Several short, clean sessions are stronger than one long, noisy recording. Between sessions, log what changed: who entered or left, whether HVAC changed, whether a door opened, whether an appliance started, or whether outside noise increased.

Use more than one recorder when possible. Multiple recorders create context. A sound captured strongly on one recorder but not another nearby recorder does not prove origin, but it may help evaluate distance, direction, device sensitivity, obstruction, and local sound sources.

End the session clearly. State that the session is ending, give the time, and stop the recorder. After the session, verify that the file saved correctly, preserve the original, and create working copies for review.

Audio Contamination and Forensic Limits

Audio is powerful because it can preserve sounds people did not notice in the moment. Audio is also easy to mishear, over-process, or detach from context. A voice-like sound should be treated as a question, not an answer.

Contamination should be assumed until reasonably ruled out. Common sources include investigator whispering, clothing movement, recorder handling, tripod vibration, footsteps, HVAC changes, plumbing, wind, rain, distant traffic, neighbors, animals, phone notifications, radio bleed, television sound, and voices carrying through walls, windows, vents, or stairwells.

If multiple recorders were placed in different rooms, compare them. A sound captured equally on several recorders may be a loud environmental sound rather than a local anomaly. A sound captured by one recorder but not by another nearby recorder requires careful documentation, but still does not establish origin by itself.

Do not lead reviewers. If a clip sounds like speech, do not tell another listener what phrase to hear. Provide the time range and ask what, if anything, they hear. If reviewers independently report similar content without prompting, the clip may deserve further review. If reviewers hear different phrases, ambiguity or auditory pareidolia should be considered.

Enhancement must be logged. If a copy is amplified, filtered, noise-reduced, equalized, slowed, normalized, or clipped, record the software, settings, file name, and reason for the change. Always compare the processed version to the original. If a word only appears after aggressive processing, the responsible conclusion is caution, not certainty.

Reviewing Photographs

Photographs are among the most commonly collected forms of evidence and among the most commonly misinterpreted. A camera captures light, not conclusions. Every anomaly in a photograph should be examined against ordinary causes before any unusual interpretation is considered.

Lighting is one of the most important factors in photographic review. Note flash, ambient room lights, windows, candles, equipment lights, and any other light sources. Light can create reflections, lens flares, shadows, and color casts that look unusual.

Reflections are common sources of false positives. Glass, polished surfaces, water, eyeglasses, camera screens, mirrors, and lens surfaces can create figures, faces, or lights that were not actually present as independent objects.

Motion blur can make people, objects, insects, or the camera itself appear as streaks, smears, translucent shapes, or partial figures. Slow shutter speed in low light should always be checked before assigning meaning to a blurred image.

Dust and airborne particles are common causes of orbs in flash or infrared photography. Dust, pollen, pet dander, hair, lint, insulation fibers, ash, drywall dust, carpet fibers, and other particles can pass close to the lens and catch light. The camera is not showing the particle's real-world size. It is showing how that particle interacted with light, focus, distance, and the lens.

Insects near the lens can create anomalies that look very different from dust. A moth, fly, gnat, or other insect caught in flash or infrared light can appear as a streak, blur, rod, irregular shape, or glowing form. Warm weather and outdoor locations increase this possibility.

Camera settings matter. ISO, shutter speed, aperture, white balance, focus mode, flash status, and automatic processing all affect how the image appears. Before interpreting any anomaly, review the settings and consider how the device captured the scene.

A single photograph is rarely enough to support a strong conclusion. Compare nearby images in the sequence. An anomaly that appears only once may be a transient artifact. An anomaly that appears across multiple frames deserves more attention, but still requires evaluation against natural causes.

Visual Metadata, Artifacts, and Context

Photo and video review should begin before anyone zooms in on the strange-looking area. Start with context. What device captured the image? Was it a phone, DSLR, action camera, security camera, trail camera, webcam, infrared camera, night-vision camera, or still frame exported from video?

Each device has limitations. Phone cameras apply automatic processing. Security cameras compress heavily. Night-vision footage can exaggerate dust and insects. Wide-angle lenses distort edges. Infrared illumination can bounce from nearby surfaces. A visual anomaly cannot be judged fairly without knowing the device that created it.

Metadata is part of the evidence when available. It may include date, time, device model, exposure, ISO, shutter speed, focal length, flash status, image dimensions, GPS information, and software history. Metadata is not perfect, and it can be missing or altered, but it is still valuable context.

Compression can create false structure. Many images and videos are compressed to reduce file size. Compression can create blocks, smears, edge halos, false texture, and color banding. When viewers zoom into compressed images, those artifacts can begin to look like faces, writing, figures, eyes, or symbols.

Night footage requires special caution. Infrared cameras, low-light sensors, and automatic exposure systems can turn ordinary movement into dramatic-looking footage. Dust, pollen, insects, moisture, breath vapor, spiderwebs, reflective tape, shiny surfaces, and moving shadows can all appear strange under infrared illumination.

Use comparison frames. For video, examine the seconds before and after the event. For photographs, compare nearby images in the sequence. Does the light source change? Does the camera shift? Does a team member move nearby? Does the object appear only during a compression change?

Do not crop away the explanation. Cropping can help show a detail, but the full frame must remain available. A cropped image may remove the window that caused a reflection, the flashlight beam that caused a shadow, the person at the edge of the frame, or the object that created the blur.

Spiderwebs, Orbs, and Fixed-Camera False Positives

Fixed cameras deserve special caution because they watch a scene continuously. Trail cameras, security cameras, GoPros, doorbell cameras, and static investigation cameras may capture the same patch of air for hours, days, or weeks. Over time, spiderwebs, dust, pollen, dander, rain droplets, condensation, insects, leaves, grass, straps, cables, and reflections can move into the lens area.

A camera that looked clear at setup may not be clear later. A spider may build a web across the field of view after the team leaves. A thin strand can drift in air currents and look like a white streak, moving line, reaching shape, or leg-like motion. Under infrared light, those strands can glow brightly and appear more solid than they are.

Trail cameras are designed to detect movement and capture wildlife, not to interpret paranormal claims. Outdoors, they are surrounded by insects, leaves, rain, pollen, seed fluff, snow, fog, branches, grasses, webbing, and animals. A trigger without an obvious visible source should not be treated as automatic evidence of an invisible entity.

Security and doorbell cameras often use wide-angle lenses, low resolution, heavy compression, automatic exposure, night vision, motion detection, and cloud processing. Porch lights, insects near IR emitters, rain on the lens, headlights, and web strands across the housing can create dramatic artifacts.

Action cameras have wide-angle lenses and small sensors. Their geometry can distort edges, stretch shapes, exaggerate motion, and make objects near the lens appear unusual. Mounts, straps, cables, sleeves, hair, and tripod shadows should be checked when reviewing this footage.

Look for attachment points. Webbing, hair, fiber, plant material, string, or loose cable may pivot from a corner, stretch from the top edge of the frame, or return to the same resting position. If an anomaly moves like it is hinged, tethered, swaying, or returning to one place, ordinary physical causes should be considered first.

Orbs need ordinary explanations first. Dust, pollen, dander, moisture droplets, insects, and tiny fibers can reflect flash or infrared light. The closer the particle is to the lens, the larger and softer it may appear. Orbs that drift with airflow, appear in clusters, show up only with flash or IR, or appear frequently in dusty rooms should be treated as environmental particulate unless stronger evidence says otherwise.

Setup and pickup checks matter. Inspect cameras when placing them and when collecting them. Document lens condition, nearby lights, reflective surfaces, vents, windows, branches, webbing, loose materials, possible moving objects, and any changes observed at pickup. The pickup check is often where the explanation is found.

Reviewing Video

Video review combines the challenges of audio and photographic review with the added complexity of motion and timing. A video recording captures a sequence of events, and reviewing it properly requires attention to both the visual and audio tracks.

If multiple cameras were used, review them in synchronization when possible. Compare time codes to see whether an event captured by one camera was captured by another. A shadow that appears on one camera but not another covering the same area may be a camera artifact, angle issue, or localized light effect.

Most movement captured during an investigation has ordinary causes. Curtains move from air currents. Plants sway from drafts. Dust particles float. Shadows shift as lights move or clouds pass. Before interpreting motion as anomalous, check vents, open windows, fans, traffic, pedestrian movement, and team activity.

Shadows are frequent false positives. A shadow that appears to move on its own may be caused by a changing light source, a person outside the frame, or automatic exposure adjustment. Trace the light source before interpreting the shadow.

Camera shake can make stationary objects appear to move. Handheld cameras, unstable mounts, wooden floors, wind, vibration, and people walking nearby can shift a frame. If the camera was not securely mounted, account for movement before interpreting the footage.

Compression artifacts are common in digital video. Blocky areas, color banding, ghosting around moving objects, and pixelation are produced by compression algorithms. Low-light footage, high-motion scenes, and heavily compressed files are especially vulnerable.

Frame-by-frame review is useful when a moment appears unusual after normal playback. It can reveal details that are invisible at full speed. A shape that appears to be a figure may resolve into shadow, reflection, insect movement, or camera artifact when examined frame by frame.

Organization, File Naming, and Backups

Evidence review cannot begin properly until media is organized. A pile of files named IMG_0423.JPG or AUDIO001.WAV is not a usable evidence system. Organization is the foundation that makes review possible.

A good file name should include useful context such as date, location, device, area, subject, time, or investigator initials. A consistent naming convention allows files to be sorted, searched, and correlated with logs.

Create a folder structure for each investigation. A practical structure separates raw media, audio, photographs, video, processed copies, reports, and supporting notes. Original files should remain separate from working copies.

Synchronize device clocks before the investigation whenever possible. A time difference of even a few minutes can make correlation difficult. Cameras, recorders, phones, tablets, and environmental tools should be aligned to the same reference time.

Back up media immediately. Original files should be preserved in at least two locations when possible. Hard drives fail, memory cards are lost, and files can be deleted accidentally. Evidence integrity depends on preservation.

Metadata should be preserved. Digital files may contain date, time, device, camera settings, and software history. Metadata can be altered or removed through processing, so original files should remain untouched.

Chain of Custody for Media Files

Chain of custody means knowing where the file came from, who handled it, what was done to it, and where the original is stored. Paranormal investigators may not be law enforcement, but evidence integrity still matters.

Files should be transferred from each device into the case folder using a consistent process. Do not scatter files across personal desktops, phone galleries, text messages, and random folders. Record who performed the transfer, when it happened, and which device the files came from.

Separate original, working, and presentation files. The original file is the untouched file copied from the capture device. The working file is a duplicate used for listening, viewing, clipping, or analysis. The presentation file is a version prepared for a report, client meeting, archive, or educational discussion.

Log every derivative. If a clip is created from a longer recording, record the source file, start time, end time, export name, and reason for the clip. If a photo is brightened, write down the software and adjustment. If a still frame is extracted, document the original video file and exact timestamp.

Protect client privacy. Residential recordings may include family conversations, children, medical details, personal photographs, religious items, financial papers, or private emotional moments. Business recordings may include employees, customers, security procedures, inventory, or confidential information. Evidence review should happen inside the case team, not in public chats or social-media threads.

Do not let excitement break the chain. A strange clip should not be immediately sent to friends or posted online. The stronger habit is to pause, preserve, log, compare, review, and then decide whether the file is appropriate to share under the case's permissions.

Comparing Media and Correlation

One piece of data is rarely conclusive. A sound, image, or temperature reading becomes more useful when compared with other records from the same time and location.

Compare audio to investigation notes. Did anyone report hearing the same sound at the same time? Was there movement nearby? Did the field log document HVAC, plumbing, outdoor noise, animal activity, or team movement?

Compare photographs to logs. What was the image intended to capture? Were flash, infrared, or low-light settings used? Was the anomaly visible to the photographer at the time? Were reflective surfaces, dust, insects, or moisture present?

Compare video to environmental conditions. Did the visual event align with a temperature change, EMF reading, pressure shift, team movement, or lighting change? Correlating footage with environmental data may identify natural causes.

Compare witness statements to media. A witness who reported hearing a knock at a specific time should be compared to audio from that time. A witness who reported seeing a figure in a specific location should be compared to footage from that area.

Correlation is not causation. Two events happening together may be meaningful, or they may simply share timing. Correlation is a starting point for further analysis, not a conclusion by itself.

Review Roles and Independent Checks

A serious review process works better when roles are defined. Evidence review should not depend on one person sitting alone with headphones and a strong opinion.

The primary reviewer performs the first complete pass through assigned files. This person documents timestamps, obvious explanations, questionable moments, and files that need a second look.

The secondary reviewer checks flagged items without being told what conclusion to reach. For audio, the secondary reviewer should ideally listen without a suggested phrase. For visual media, they should view the full frame or clip before being shown a crop or label.

The case lead compares reviewer notes against the field log, environmental log, equipment log, and witness statements. This person determines whether the file is explained, likely explained, unresolved, unusable, or needs advanced review.

A respectful skeptic role is useful. This is not the person who mocks the case. It is the person assigned to ask what else could explain the file: reflection, compression, movement, animal sound, outside voices, plumbing, HVAC, device noise, camera processing, timing error, or another ordinary cause.

Disagreement should be documented. If one reviewer hears "help me" and another hears a mechanical squeak, write both down. If one reviewer sees a figure and another sees shadow texture, write both down. Disagreement clarifies the limits of the evidence.

Avoiding Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to notice, remember, and interpret information in a way that confirms what a person already believes. It is not a character flaw. It is how the human brain works. Every investigator must guard against it.

Expectation shapes review. If a reviewer expects to find paranormal evidence, ambiguous sounds may become voices and random patterns may become faces. If a reviewer expects to find nothing, meaningful anomalies may be dismissed too quickly. The best review posture is disciplined neutrality.

Selective review creates distortion. Reviewing only interesting files or only flagged moments creates a biased sample. Uneventful files and quiet periods are also data.

Replay fatigue changes perception. Listening to a section of audio or watching a clip repeatedly can make ambiguous material seem clearer than it is. If a reviewer is trying to force meaning from a clip, they should step away and return later with fresh ears or eyes.

Auditory pareidolia is the tendency to hear patterns in random noise. Static, wind, running water, fans, and background hiss can all sound speech-like. Visual pareidolia is the tendency to see familiar patterns, especially faces or figures, in random visual information. Both must be considered during review.

Responsible Interpretation

The most important principle in media review is order: observation first, explanation second, conclusion last.

Observation describes exactly what was heard, seen, or measured. "Three tapping sounds at 00:14:32" is an observation. "A dark shape appears in the upper left corner" is an observation. "The temperature reading dropped from 68 to 62 degrees over 30 seconds" is an observation.

Explanation comes after observation. Start with ordinary causes. Could the sound be plumbing? Could the shape be a shadow? Could the temperature drop be HVAC? Could the voice-like sound be contamination, radio interference, or auditory pareidolia?

Conclusions come last. A conclusion may be natural explanation identified, likely explained, inconclusive, unresolved, needs further review, possible, or plausible. The language should match the strength of the evidence.

Responsible interpretation requires patience. It requires sitting with uncertainty and not forcing a conclusion before the evidence supports one. "I do not know" is a valid conclusion when the evidence does not support stronger language.

Practical Review Scenarios

The unexplained sound. During audio review, an investigator hears a low thud in a hallway recording. The notes show a team member walking between adjacent rooms at that time. The sound is explained by movement.

The blurred figure. A basement photograph shows a human-shaped blur near stairs. The camera settings show a slow shutter speed, and the photo log notes the photographer was moving. The likely cause is motion blur.

The reflected light. A video shows a bright moving light in a dark room. The camera faces a window, and footage shows a car outside. The likely cause is headlight reflection.

The camera movement. A stationary camera appears to shift. Review shows the camera was on a lightweight tripod on a wooden floor and moved slightly when people walked nearby.

The insect near the lens. An outdoor flash photograph shows a large irregular anomaly on a warm night. Other photos from the same time show similar shapes. The likely cause is an insect close to the lens.

The unresolved audio. A voice-like sound appears in a quiet room and does not correspond to known team movement, HVAC, windows, or radio sources. Independent reviewers agree that it is unusual but do not establish origin. The responsible classification is unresolved audio anomaly requiring further review, not proof of communication.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Reviewing only interesting files creates a biased sample. All files should be reviewed or accounted for, including uneventful recordings.

Editing originals destroys the primary evidence source. Always work from copies and preserve the original file untouched.

Sharing before analysis can mislead the public, compromise privacy, and weaken the case. Analyze first. Share later, if appropriate and permitted.

Ignoring notes removes context. Media should be reviewed alongside field notes, environmental logs, equipment logs, and witness observations.

Reviewing alone increases the risk of bias. Independent review helps identify ambiguity and prevents one interpretation from becoming the official conclusion too quickly.

Over-enhancing media can create artifacts that look or sound like evidence. Minimal processing, documented settings, and comparison to the original are essential.

Common Misconceptions

"Every anomaly is paranormal." An anomaly is simply something that cannot be immediately explained. Most anomalies have ordinary causes that become apparent through careful review.

"Clearer always means better." Enhancement can introduce artifacts, remove details, and create a false impression of clarity. The original file remains the most accurate record of what was captured.

"One photograph proves a haunting." A single photograph is a data point, not a conclusion. Lighting, reflections, dust, insects, compression, focus, and processing must all be considered.

"Audio enhancement creates truth." Amplifying or filtering audio does not reveal certainty. It reveals what the processing produces. If words appear only after aggressive processing, the finding requires caution.

"All static contains voices." The brain looks for patterns in noise. Static, hiss, white noise, and background sound can be interpreted as speech through auditory pareidolia. Most static is simply static.

Conclusion

Audio, photographs, and video are valuable only when they are preserved, contextualized, reviewed, and interpreted responsibly. The strength of media evidence does not come from how dramatic it appears. It comes from whether the original file was protected, whether contamination was considered, whether ordinary causes were tested, and whether the conclusion matches what the file actually supports.

Media review is where excitement must give way to discipline. The unknown deserves investigation, but every file must first be allowed to tell the truth of what it actually contains.
