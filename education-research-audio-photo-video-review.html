Introduction to Audio, Photo, and Video Review

An evidence-analysis paper on reviewing media with context, restraint, and preservation of original files.

Evidence Science and Analysis Reporting and Documentation

Abstract

Audio, photographs, and video are among the most common forms of evidence presented in paranormal investigation. They are also among the easiest to misinterpret when context is missing.

This paper introduces responsible media review, including original-file preservation, contamination checks, and careful interpretation.

Original Files Matter

The original file is the evidence source. Edited clips, compressed social-media uploads, screenshots, and enhanced versions may be useful for discussion, but they should not replace the original.

Preserving metadata, timestamps, device information, and recording context helps investigators evaluate what was captured.

Audio Review

Audio should be reviewed with awareness of team movement, whispering, clothing noise, breath sounds, HVAC, plumbing, distant voices, radio interference, and recorder handling.

Excessive filtering can create artifacts. Basic cleanup may help clarity, but every change should be documented and compared against the original.

Photo and Video Review

Photographs and video should be evaluated for flash reflection, dust, insects, moisture, motion blur, lens artifacts, compression, low-light noise, reflections, shadows, and focus issues.

Frame-by-frame review can be useful, but investigators should avoid forcing meaning into ambiguous shapes.

Conclusion

Media review is not about finding the most exciting interpretation. It is about determining what the file actually supports. Careful review protects both the evidence and the conclusion.
