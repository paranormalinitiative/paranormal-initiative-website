The Mistake Investigators Must Avoid

One of the biggest mistakes made in paranormal investigation is assuming that anything unusual must have an extraordinary explanation.

In reality, some of the most convincing "paranormal" photographs ever captured turn out to be something far more ordinary.

The images shown here are an excellent example.

What The Images Appear To Show

At first glance, many people might describe them as fairies, spirits, energy beings, rods, or other unexplained entities.

In reality, they are insects.

More specifically, they are insects photographed at night using a camera with flash. As they fly close to the lens, the flash overexposes them while motion blur and wing movement create shapes that our brains naturally try to interpret.

Pareidolia And Pattern Recognition

This is known as pareidolia: our tendency to recognize familiar shapes and patterns in random visual information.

It is one of the reasons responsible investigators do not stop at the first explanation that feels exciting.

They investigate further.

Examination Before Conclusion

This does not mean every unexplained photograph has a simple answer. It means every photograph deserves to be examined before a conclusion is reached.

Slow the video down. Review it frame by frame. Look for wings. Look for legs. Look for body structure.

Consider the lighting. Consider the environment. Ask what camera settings were used. And most importantly, ask yourself what the evidence actually supports.

The Burden Of Proof

The burden of proof always belongs to the person making the claim.

If someone claims a photograph shows a fairy, spirit, or unknown entity, then that conclusion should be supported by careful analysis, not simply by how unusual the image appears.

The goal of paranormal investigation is not to prove something extraordinary. The goal is to determine what is actually there.

The Truth Is Worth Finding

Sometimes the answer is remarkable.

Sometimes it is surprisingly ordinary.

Either way, the truth is always worth finding.

Follow the evidence. Not the belief.
