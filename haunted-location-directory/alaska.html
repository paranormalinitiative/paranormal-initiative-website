Alaska

11 Locations

Alaska contains some of the most geographically isolated and environmentally complex documented sites in North America. From Gold Rush-era structures in Southeast Alaska to Cold War military facilities and deep wilderness zones, the state demands rigorous environmental baseline assessment alongside standard TPI documentation protocols. Remote access conditions, extreme climate variables, and significant cultural sensitivity factors apply across this region.

Golden North Hotel

Hotel Haunted Historic
Skagway, AK
Historic Hotel
Access: Public

Klondike Gold Rush era hotel established 1898, among the oldest hotels in Alaska. Reported activity has centered primarily on upper-floor rooms and narrow corridor spaces. The figure known locally as "Mary" — associated with a Gold Rush-era resident who reportedly died awaiting the return of her fiance — has been documented across multiple independent investigator accounts spanning decades. Hallway acoustics in the original structure represent a required baseline calibration factor.

Activity Reported
  • Apparition of a woman in period dress observed on upper floors
  • Phantom footsteps in corridor spaces
  • Doors opening without cause in sealed rooms
  • Reported presence sensation in attic level
  • Acoustic anomalies consistent with narrow hallway resonance
  • Cold spots near third-floor room entries
Structure Layout

  • Attic Level (restricted access)

Red Dog Saloon

Saloon Haunted Historic
Juneau, AK
Historic Saloon
Access: Public

Gold Rush-era saloon established 1899. Frequented by miners, prospectors, and reportedly Wyatt Earp during the Klondike era. The venue's proximity to an active waterfront and its continuous operation as a high-traffic public space make environmental noise calibration a required baseline step. Object displacement and apparition reports have been documented primarily near the main bar and stage area across multiple independent accounts.

Activity Reported

  • Phantom piano and instrumental music during off-hours
  • Objects reportedly displaced without contact
  • Cold drafts in sealed areas without ventilation source
  • Shadow movement observed near the back wall
  • Disembodied voices reported by staff during quiet periods

Buckner Building

Military Haunted Abandoned
Whittier, AK
Abandoned Military Structure
Access: Restricted

Massive Cold War-era military housing facility constructed 1953. At peak capacity, the Buckner Building housed the entire civilian and military population of Whittier. Abandoned since 1969, the structure has accumulated significant environmental decay. Note: environmental hazards including structural instability, deteriorating materials, and confined air quality issues make unauthorized entry dangerous. Reported activity spans multiple floors and must be evaluated against substantial environmental contamination factors before any classification can be confirmed.

Activity Reported
  • Shadow figures in long abandoned corridor stretches
  • Disembodied voices attributed to former residents
  • Reports of children's sounds in abandoned residential areas
  • Heavy oppressive atmospheric sensation on upper floors
  • Unexplained light phenomena in sealed units
  • Structural and environmental sound require careful documentation control
Structure Layout
  • Main Entry / Ground Level
  • Multi-floor Residential Corridors (floors 1-6)
  • Abandoned Unit Interiors
  • Stairwells (multiple — structurally compromised)
  • Mechanical Basement Levels
  • Exterior Platforms (deteriorated)

Kennecott Mines

Industrial Haunted Historic
McCarthy, AK
National Historic Landmark
Access: Public (NPS)

Active copper mining operation from 1903 to 1938, Kennecott processed some of the richest ore deposits ever discovered in North America. The site, now administered by the National Park Service as a National Historic Landmark, consists of multi-story mill buildings, worker housing, and processing facilities preserved largely as found at closure. Decades of hazardous mining labor under extreme conditions have produced what investigators describe as a densely imprinted site environment.

Activity Reported
  • Phantom footsteps in mill building stairwells
  • Reported miner apparitions in period work clothing
  • Metallic sounds from inactive machinery areas
  • Disembodied voices in former worker housing
  • Cold spots documented in lower processing levels
  • Shadow figures observed on upper mill floors
Structure Layout
  • Concentration Mill — multi-floor (primary activity zone)
  • Ammonia Leaching Plant
  • Worker Housing Barracks
  • Power House
  • General Store and Town Buildings
  • Underground Tunnel Access Points (NPS restricted)
Location Data

Castle Hill

Historic Haunted Government
Sitka, AK
State Historic Site
Access: Public

Castle Hill is the elevated rocky prominence where the formal transfer of Alaska from Russia to the United States took place on October 18, 1867. Prior to American acquisition, it served as the site of the Tlingit village Noow Tlein and later the Russian colonial administrative center. The site carries layered cultural significance across indigenous, Russian colonial, and American periods. Activity reports are primarily environmental in nature and concentrated around the formal transfer site at the summit.

Activity Reported
  • Historical apparitions in period dress from multiple eras observed at summit
  • Environmental anomalies near the flag transfer site
  • Cold spots at the summit during otherwise calm weather conditions
  • Unexplained auditory phenomena consistent with ceremonial crowd sounds
  • Shadow figures near Russian-era foundation remnants
  • Atmospheric pressure anomalies documented at the highest point
Structure Layout
  • Summit Grounds (primary activity zone)

Tongass National Forest

Wilderness Haunted Regional
Southeast Alaska Region
Wilderness / Regional Anomaly Entry
Access: Varies by zone

Encompassing approximately 17 million acres, the Tongass is the largest national forest in the United States. This entry is classified as a regional anomaly entry rather than a standard location, documenting indigenous cultural accounts, folklore traditions, and field-reported wilderness anomalies across the forest region. The Kushtaka (Land Otter Man) of Tlingit tradition represents one of the most substantiated indigenous entity accounts in North American documentation, with consistent behavioral descriptors recorded across centuries of separate cultural testimony.

Activity Reported
  • Mimicry voice phenomena reported by wilderness travelers
  • Shape-shifting entity reports consistent with Kushtaka behavioral descriptors
  • Documented disappearance incidents in remote zones (causes unconfirmed)
  • Disorientation and navigation failure in otherwise clear conditions
  • Sound anomalies inconsistent with known regional wildlife
  • Reported encounters concentrated near water features and river confluences
Structure Layout
  • Coastal Forest Zones
  • River Confluence Points (historically elevated activity)
  • Remote Wilderness Interior
  • Island Waterways (Alexander Archipelago)
  • Trail Corridors (limited access areas)
Location Data
