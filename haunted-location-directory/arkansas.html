Arkansas

12 Locations

Arkansas contains some of the most well-documented and consistently reported paranormal activity in the southern United States. From Victorian-era resort hotels and frontier-era execution sites to Civil War battlefields, mining ghost towns, and private investigation properties, the state presents a layered investigation environment requiring careful historical grounding and baseline control across diverse site types.

Crescent Hotel

Hotel Haunted Historic
Eureka Springs, AR
Public Hotel
Access: Public

Built in 1886 as a luxury resort hotel, the Crescent has operated continuously across multiple distinct eras that have contributed significantly to its documented investigation history. Most critically, from 1937 to 1939 the building was repurposed by Norman Baker as a fraudulent cancer treatment hospital — a period in which patients with terminal illness paid substantial sums for treatments later found to have no medical basis, and in which numerous patients died on the premises. This hospital era is identified as the primary contributing factor to reported activity. The site is widely considered one of the most heavily investigated hotels in the United States, with a substantial body of independent investigation records accumulated across decades.

Activity Reported
  • Apparition identified as "Michael" — a stonemason reportedly killed during construction — documented in basement and lower areas
  • Nurse figure apparition ("Theodora") reported in former hospital corridors
  • Child apparition reported on the third floor across multiple independent accounts
  • Multiple distinct entity types documented across different floor zones
  • Hundreds of independent guest and investigator experience reports on record
  • Object movement and cold spots in former patient room areas

Basin Park Hotel

Hotel Haunted Historic
Eureka Springs, AR
Victorian Hotel
Access: Public

Built in 1905 and set directly into the Ozark hillside, the Basin Park Hotel is unusual among Arkansas investigation sites in that each of its seven floors opens at ground level on a different hillside tier. Reports of paranormal activity have been documented by guests and investigators across multiple decades, with a particular concentration of accounts tied to the rooftop areas, basement cave zones, and specific upper-floor corridors. The site's layered geological and architectural character creates environmental variables requiring careful baseline control.

Fort Smith National Historic Site

Military Haunted Historic
Fort Smith, AR
National Park Service
Access: Public

Frontier military fort established 1817, later converted to a federal court under Judge Isaac Parker — "The Hanging Judge" — whose jurisdiction oversaw 86 documented executions by hanging between 1875 and 1896. The site encompasses both the original military fort remains and the federal court complex, including the preserved gallows structure, jail cells, and courtroom. The density of violent and judicial death events associated with the site creates a high-potential imprinting environment, with activity reports concentrated around execution-era areas. NPS access rules govern all investigation activity.

Activity Reported
  • Phantom horse and cavalry sounds on fort grounds
  • Apparition associated with the hangman or condemned figures near the gallows
  • Disembodied voices in the jail and courtroom areas
  • Cold spots concentrated at the gallows structure
  • Footsteps in the preserved interior spaces
  • Unexplained sounds in the basement jail area after hours
Structure Layout

  • Jail / Basement Cell Areas (primary activity zone)

Old State House Museum

Museum Haunted Historic
Little Rock, AR
Historic Capitol / Museum
Access: Public

The original Arkansas state capitol, built in 1836 and serving as the seat of state government until 1911. The building witnessed significant political conflict across its occupancy, including dueling incidents in the legislative chambers, multiple deaths associated with its history, and occupation and use by both sides during the Civil War. Now operating as a museum, paranormal reports are driven primarily by visitor accounts and longstanding folklore associated with the building's turbulent political era. The museum context means investigation access requires institutional authorization.

Activity Reported

  • Woman in black figure documented near legislative rooms

  • Door anomalies in unoccupied sections
  • Cold spots in the original legislative chamber area

Arlington Resort Hotel & Spa

Hotel Haunted Historic
Hot Springs, AR
Historic Resort Hotel
Access: Public

The Arlington has anchored Hot Springs' Bathhouse Row since the original structure opened in 1875, with the current building completed in 1924. The hotel was a favored destination for gangsters, politicians, and celebrities during the Prohibition and Depression eras, and has operated continuously for over a century. Its long uninterrupted occupancy across multiple significant historical periods creates layered conditions for investigation. Activity reports have come primarily from overnight guests and long-term staff, concentrated in the guest floor corridors and ballroom-level areas.

Clayton House

Museum Haunted Historic
Fort Smith, AR
Historic Mansion / Museum
Access: Public (Tour Hours)

Built in 1882 as the residence of U.S. Attorney William H. H. Clayton, who prosecuted cases in Judge Isaac Parker's federal court during the height of frontier-era enforcement in Indian Territory. The Victorian mansion has been preserved and operates as a historic museum. The house's direct connection to the judicial and law-enforcement apparatus responsible for the Fort Smith executions creates a contextual link between this site and the National Historic Site a short distance away. Activity reports are concentrated in the staircase hall and upper room areas.

Gurdon Light

Railway Haunted Regional
Gurdon, AR (Clark County)
Outdoor Anomaly / Railway Corridor
Access: Public (trail access)

One of Arkansas's most documented recurring anomaly phenomena, the Gurdon Light is a floating light reported moving along the railroad tracks near Gurdon in Clark County. The light has been observed and documented over many decades by independent witnesses. Local folklore attributes the light to a railroad worker killed on the tracks, though no definitive scientific explanation has been established. This entry is classified as an outdoor recurring anomaly rather than a standard building investigation entry. Access is via a hiking trail and the phenomenon requires patience and appropriate weather conditions for observation.

Activity Reported
  • Recurring floating light phenomenon observed along the rail corridor
  • Light movement along the track with apparent directional behavior
  • Brightness and apparent distance shifts during observation
  • Strong local legend associations with a railroad worker death
  • Multiple independent witness accounts spanning decades

Pea Ridge National Military Park

Military Haunted Historic
Garfield, AR
National Military Park (NPS)
Access: Public

Site of the Battle of Pea Ridge (March 7–8, 1862), one of the largest and most decisive Civil War engagements west of the Mississippi. The two-day battle resulted in significant casualties on both sides and secured Missouri for the Union. The preserved battlefield encompasses the Elkhorn Tavern — used as headquarters by both armies at different points in the battle — along with artillery positions, troop movement corridors, and combat-associated terrain. NPS access regulations govern all activity on the grounds.

Activity Reported

  • Cold spots in the Elkhorn Tavern interior

Rush Historic District

Industrial Haunted Historic
Near Yellville, AR (Buffalo National River)
Mining Ghost Town / NPS
Access: Public (NPS trails)

Rush was an active zinc mining community from the 1880s through the 1960s, with a peak population of several hundred residents during the World War I zinc boom. The town was gradually abandoned as zinc prices declined, and the physical remains — including mine structures, ruins of commercial buildings, and a former hotel — are now preserved as part of Buffalo National River. The isolated setting, structural decay, and multi-generational mining history create both significant environmental hazards and a rich investigation environment. Access is via NPS-maintained trails; structural entry requires safety assessment.

Activity Reported
  • Footsteps in abandoned building areas without identifiable cause
  • Environmental unease and atmospheric pressure changes reported by visitors
  • Mining-era residual lore associated with specific structure sites
  • Structure-associated sounds from vacant buildings
  • Cold spots in the more intact building remains

Allen House

Residence Haunted Historic
Monticello, AR
Historic Residence
Access: Limited

A three-story Colonial Revival house built in 1906 for Joe Lee Allen, a prominent local businessman. The home is associated most strongly with the death of Ladell Allen, who died in the house in 1948 in circumstances that were the subject of ongoing speculation. Activity reports have accumulated across multiple decades and include apparitional sightings, voice phenomena, and room-specific recurring disturbances. The home's original architecture and multi-generational residential history contribute to its layered investigation profile.
