Types of Hauntings and Claim Categories

A classification paper for organizing reports without forcing conclusions.

Investigation Science Historical and Cultural Research

Abstract

Reported paranormal activity is often grouped into categories such as residual, intelligent or interactive, environmental, crisis-related, apparition-based, poltergeist-type, or psychologically influenced experiences.

This paper explains why categories are useful as organizational tools but should not be treated as conclusions before evidence is reviewed.

Categories as Working Models

A category helps investigators organize claims. Repeating footsteps at the same time may suggest a residual pattern. Responses that appear connected to questions may suggest possible interaction. Moving objects may suggest a physical-disturbance claim.

However, a category is not proof of cause. It is a working model that guides what questions should be asked next.

Environmental and Natural Categories

Many reported hauntings are later connected to structural movement, airflow, lighting, sound transmission, wildlife, electrical sources, psychological factors, or social expectation. Environmental causes should always remain an active category, not an afterthought.

Recognizing environmental categories does not dismiss anomalous possibilities. It prevents investigators from mislabeling ordinary causes as paranormal.

Unresolved and Possible Findings

When a claim cannot be explained after careful review, it may be documented as unresolved. If the evidence is consistent, contextual, and supported by multiple observations, the investigator may consider possible or plausible anomalous classification.

The key is sequence: observe, document, compare, test, then classify.

Conclusion

Types of hauntings and claim categories help organize investigation, but they must not replace investigation. The category should emerge from the evidence, not be imposed on it.
